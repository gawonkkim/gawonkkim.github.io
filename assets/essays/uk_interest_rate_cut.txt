The article outlines the UK government's benchmark interest rate cut by 0.25%, reaching its lowest in two years at 4% from 4.25%. This raises concerns of slowing economic growth alongside persistent inflation, as the UK has the highest inflation rate in the G7, marking 3.8%. The UK economy has been weakening — GDP growth has been slowing from 0.7% in Q1 2025 to 0.3% in Q3 2025, alongside employment contracting by 26,000 in June and 8,000 in July. Hence, the UK chancellor faces difficult trade-offs, creating a policy dilemma to balance inflation and growth. The key concept of sustainability frames this analysis, as sustainability is the ability to meet current needs and wants without generating unsustainable obligations that compromise future generations' well-being. Decreasing interest rates may allow households to enjoy more goods and services in the short run. However, persistent inflationary pressure can compromise long-term growth, raising concern for financial sustainability for the UK.

An expansionary monetary policy is a demand-side policy that the central bank implements to manipulate aggregate demand by controlling the supply of money and interest rates to achieve economic goals. This stimulates economic growth and lowers unemployment due to the decreased borrowing costs.

The UK government increased its money supply, causing the interest rate to fall from 4.25% to 4%. In an AD-AS diagram, the market equilibrium was initially at the intersection of the long-run aggregate supply, short-run aggregate supply, and the original aggregate demand curve. However, due to the decrease in interest rates, aggregate demand increased, shifting the AD curve to the right. This altered the equilibrium, and real GDP — the UK's output — increased in the short run, showing higher investment and spending. However, since long-run aggregate supply is fixed, meaning long-run growth isn't affected, the price level rising beyond the original equilibrium reflects inflation.

The decision to cut interest rates offers apparent short-run benefits but also long-run disadvantages. In the short run, this policy is effective, as the 0.25 percentage point interest rate cut allows aggregate demand to boost during a period of slow GDP growth (0.3%) and declining employment. As borrowing costs decrease, households and firms tend to spend more, which may balance the downturn and lower the unemployment rate. Additionally, inflation in the UK economy has steadily fallen from a peak of 11% in 2022, which justifies the decision to cut interest rates to stimulate aggregate demand.

However, inflation is at 3.8%, still significantly higher than the target of 2% and the highest in the G7. Thus, if the pound depreciates and import costs rise, this monetary policy worsens international competitiveness. Furthermore, time lags and uncertainty in confidence may interfere with its effectiveness. A change in interest rate takes time to be reflected in spending and investment, meaning inflationary pressures may persist or accelerate by the time the effects are felt. Therefore, cutting interest rates would further weaken price stability and undermine its effectiveness in the long run.

Additionally, fiscal conditions must be considered. According to the article, the UK has a 5% GDP deficit, and debt interest cost has more than doubled from six years ago. If monetary easing persists, not only will it keep inflation higher for longer as it stimulates demand, but it will also lead to investors demanding higher returns on bonds and making borrowing more expensive for the UK government. As a result, taxpayers will face a greater tax burden or receive fewer public services to reduce the debt.

In conclusion, the Bank of England's decision to cut interest rates to 4% reflects the conflict between economic growth and managing inflation, as well as sustainability. Although in the short run the expansionary monetary policy can achieve an increase in real GDP and lower unemployment, it will not be sustainable in the long run due to uncontrollable inflation. Furthermore, the UK's fiscal debt and debt interest create a risk in solely depending on demand-side policies. The UK cannot take this risk while its inflation is already the highest in the G7, as demand-pull inflation from the policy will make it worse. Therefore, despite the short-term gains, the UK should reconsider implementing this expansionary monetary policy, as the economy also depends on complementary supply-side and fiscal measures to achieve sustainable growth and price stability in the long run.
