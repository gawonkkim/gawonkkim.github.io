The article "South Korea, Malaysia Seal Free Trade Pact" reports South Korea has established a trade agreement with Malaysia, its first free trade agreement (FTA) in six years, adding its sixth FTA with an ASEAN member country. According to the Ministry of Trade, Industry, and Energy, Malaysia will cut its tariffs on Korean automobiles from 30% to 15%. Tariffs are an indirect tax imposed on imported goods, used to prevent dumping and massive current account deficits, enabling domestic producers with a comparative disadvantage to compete with foreign producers. This change in trade protection policy affects the interdependence of both nations in terms of their political relationship and international competitiveness. Interdependence in economics refers to individuals, communities and nations that are not self-sufficient and interact with each other in order to achieve economic goals — the greater the level of interaction, the greater the degree of interdependence. This commentary examines the influence on different stakeholders in Korea and Malaysia affected by this removal of tariffs and outlines how this decision increases interdependence.

The decrease in tariffs on Korean automobiles by the Malaysian government represents a classic tariff-reduction model: when a trade barrier like a tariff is removed, prices of the goods decrease. According to the article, Malaysia agreed to decrease its tariffs on Korean automobiles from 30% to 15%, which lowers the import price and is likely to increase the quantity of imports due to lower import costs. This comes at the cost of a loss in Malaysian government tariff revenue, alongside a reduction in deadweight loss compared to before the FTA. As the trade barrier is significantly decreased, both economies are likely to depend on each other more for trade, increasing their interdependence.

The tariff reductions would immediately improve South Korea's international competitiveness in the automobile industry. Korean goods will be more cost-competitive in the Malaysian market due to the halving of EV tariffs. This may increase Korea's export revenue through lower prices, increasing interaction and leading to GDP growth. For Korean automobile manufacturers, Malaysia's automobile market — the second-largest in ASEAN — offers enormous opportunities. Korea can strengthen regional supply chains by gaining access to a market previously dominated by domestic companies like Proton and Perodua, which jointly hold a 70% market share. Additionally, opportunities for foreign direct investment (FDI) are created due to the Malaysian government's decision to lift restrictions on foreign ownership in its automotive sector. Local EV assembly plants might be established by Korean companies, strengthening technological interdependence and lowering production costs through economies of scale, supporting Seoul's larger diversification strategy. Another aspect of strategic interdependence is that Korea can reduce political risk and sustain steady export demand by entering ASEAN markets as trade tensions between the US and China worsen — strengthening Korea's interdependence with ASEAN as a whole as it serves as a stepping stone for further bilateral FTAs with other member countries.

For Malaysia, the FTA will benefit domestic consumers with cheaper and more diverse options, improving cost of living and consumer choice. While incoming FDI creates jobs and transfers technology, access to Korean technology could upgrade Malaysia's automobile sector. Local automakers, however, might need structural changes to move toward higher-value production due to increased competition. Furthermore, the Malaysian government will experience a loss of tariff revenue. As a result, the agreement improves bilateral interdependence, since each nation's development becomes more tied to the other's well-being.

From an overall perspective, the FTA is a strong example of economic interdependence. Trade, investment, and production networks strengthen the ties between the growth of both countries. By diversifying Korea's commerce away from excessive reliance on the United States and China, this can improve allocative efficiency. However, interdependence also makes countries vulnerable to one another — a change in policy could have repercussions on the partner's investment flows and export performance. Additionally, both governments will lose short-term tariff revenue, which might result in unstable fiscal balances, though higher corporate and income tax revenue from increasing trade volumes may gradually offset these losses.

Overall, the Korea-Malaysia FTA's removal of tariffs on Korean automobiles and EVs carries numerous consequences. This FTA greatly benefits Korean producers and Malaysian consumers, and improves both nations' long-term growth. Furthermore, it strengthens interdependence between the two nations by removing trade barriers and promoting FDI. However, this decision also brings some harm to Malaysian producers and causes short-term losses for both nations. Hence, the FTA's success relies on how well both countries manage this interdependence for long-term gains.
