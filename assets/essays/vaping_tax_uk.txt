The article outlines a new policy announced for tax imposition on e-cigarettes, also known as 'vapes', to reduce the negative externalities of consumption starting October 2026. UK Chancellor, Rachel Reeves, decided to impose a new tax on vapes of £2.20 per 10ml of e-cigarette liquid and an equivalent increase of £2.20 per 100 cigarettes in tobacco duty, in order to not only reduce accessibility to these demerit goods, but also reduce the negative externalities of consumption, and generate revenue for public health services in the United Kingdom. Therefore, the government's decision impacts consumer choice through nudging consumers away from vaping by imposing a tax, as increasing the cost helps correcting market failure and addresses the social costs imposed by e-cigarettes by changing consumption behavior.

Changing tax policies reduces consumer choice by adjusting incentives via taxes, regulation, or subsidies. In this case, vaping is a private decision that imposes costs on the third party, such as increasing healthcare costs caused by smoking-related diseases. As seen in the market failure model, the optimal market equilibrium lies at the intersection of the marginal social cost and marginal social benefit curves. However, by smoking, the consumers not only reduce the well-being of third parties by indirect smoking but also reduce opportunity costs of funds, as the article states that the NHS is spending millions of pounds annually on patients with smoking-related conditions. However, consumers continuously consume e-cigarettes resulting in a new market equilibrium at a higher quantity than the social optimum. This results in a welfare loss due to the external cost of negative consumption externality. Failing to internalize externalities results in market inefficiencies, requiring government regulation to align consumer choice with ideal market outcomes.

When tax is imposed on demerit goods, the supply curve shifts to the left as the production of the goods is oppressed, and the producers pass the increased cost to the consumers. By increasing the price of e-cigarettes by £2.20, the equilibrium quantity is lowered, thus the market is closer to the socially optimal outcome. By addressing and internalizing the negative externalities, the new policy encourages the choice of the consumers due to taxation minimizing welfare loss and the social burden of "smoking-related conditions." However, this regulation does not remove vaping as an option; rather, it raises the cost of the choice, prompting consumers to reconsider their preferences.

To evaluate the short-term advantages of imposing a tax on cigarettes, vapes are highly inelastic in price elasticity of demand due to their addictiveness, hence many consumers will demand e-cigarettes despite price hikes. Therefore, while the tax is expected to raise large government revenue that allows for investment in public services — such as healthcare, infrastructure, or education — it may not significantly affect consumer choice as anticipated. In addition, some consumers may choose untaxed and unregulated black market options. While the tax aims to alter consumer choices, its short-term effectiveness is determined by how responsive consumers are to price changes. Thus, in this context, the reduction in consumption may be trivial due to the price change only being £2.20 per 10ml of e-cigarette liquid. Particularly, high-income or heavily addicted smokers may not be influenced as it is a small, unsubstantial proportion of their income. Therefore, the imposition of indirect tax may have only a temporary effect on consumer's choice.

In the long run, the taxation's effectiveness depends on how consumers adjust their choices over time. Consumer behaviors will shift as the government nudges consumers to stop vaping or switch to alternatives such as nicotine patches or gum. However, if the black market is involved, the regulation may fail to achieve its goals of shifting consumer choices, as untaxed goods may be an appealing option for price-sensitive consumers — or the legislation could have unforeseen consequences, such as consumers returning to regular cigarettes or tobacco, which can be more harmful. Therefore, the intention of reducing negative consumption externality may backfire with unintended consequences. While the encouragement of choice attempts to address the market failure, the policymakers must be cautious of the unintended and unforeseen disadvantages to ensure a balanced outcome over time for all stakeholders.

In conclusion, the UK vaping tax intends to influence consumer choices by raising prices of e-cigarettes, however its effectiveness is restricted due to inelastic demand and potential consequences such as increased black market activity. Thus, a balanced policy — subsidies for healthy alternatives, progressive taxation, and strong market regulations — could increase choice while lowering vape-related costs without imposing significant financial burden.
